Someone's had a dull ache in a back molar for three days. Tonight it's bad enough that they've finally pulled out their phone and searched for a dentist near them. They land on your practice's site, they like it, they click "Request an Appointment." Ready to book.

Then the form loads. Full name. Date of birth. Phone. Email. Home address. Insurance provider. Member ID. Reason for visit. Preferred date. Preferred time. Preferred provider. How did you hear about us?

They stop somewhere around the insurance ID, thumb hovering, and think: *I'll just call in the morning.*

They won't call in the morning. By morning the ache has faded to background noise and the urgency is gone. That patient — the one who was ready tonight — is gone with it. And it wasn't your prices, your reviews, or your location. It was a form that asked for too much, too soon.

## The law hiding in your intake form

There's a principle in interface design usually called *form friction*, and it rests on a simple, well-documented truth about behavior: every additional step in a process is a place where people drop out. Researchers who study checkout flows and sign-up forms see it over and over — shorten a form and completion rate climbs, lengthen it and completion rate falls. Not by a little. Field for field, it's one of the most reliable effects in all of conversion design.

The reason is cognitive load. Each field is a small task: read it, decide what it wants, retrieve the answer, type it, check it. One or two of those, fine. But stack eleven of them in front of someone who's tired, in mild pain, and half-committed, and the pile of tiny tasks starts to feel like *work*. And work is exactly what someone avoids when the easy alternative — closing the tab — costs them nothing.

## What all those fields are quietly costing you

Here's the part that should sting a little. Most of what your form asks for, you don't actually need yet.

You don't need their insurance member ID to call them back. You don't need their home address to offer a Tuesday slot. You don't need their date of birth or their preferred hygienist to start a conversation. All of that is information your front desk collects *anyway*, on the phone or at the first visit, as a normal part of intake. You've taken paperwork that belongs at the counter and moved it to the one moment when the patient is most likely to walk away.

And you never see the loss. There's no report for "started the form and quit." The person with the sore molar simply isn't on tomorrow's schedule, and you have no idea they were thirty seconds from booking.

The cruel part is that the longer form feels more professional, more thorough — so it's the natural thing to build. But thoroughness at the wrong moment reads to a nervous new patient as friction, not care.

## The fix is almost insultingly simple

Ask for the fewest things you need to start the conversation — and collect the rest later, the way you always have.

For most dental practices, "the fewest things" is three: name, phone number, and a one-line "what's going on?" That's it. That's enough for your front desk to call back, understand the situation, and book them properly. Insurance, address, date of birth, provider preference — all of that happens on that call or at the visit, where it's normal and expected and nobody abandons anything.

A good pattern is a short form up front with an optional note field, and everything else deferred to human contact. The patient feels like they've done one small, easy thing — because they have — and you've captured exactly what you need to close the loop.

A quick test you can run tonight: pull up your own appointment form on your phone and count the required fields. If it's more than four, you're almost certainly losing patients who were ready to book and lost their nerve somewhere around field seven.

## Where this leaves you

The practices winning new patients online aren't the ones with the most thorough intake forms. They're the ones that understood a ready patient is a fragile thing — briefly motivated, easily interrupted, one annoyance away from "later." They made saying yes take ten seconds instead of two minutes, and let the real paperwork happen where it always has.

Same site. Same visitors. Same practice. The only thing that changed is that booking stopped feeling like filling out forms at the DMV — and easier is what gets the person with the sore molar into your chair before the ache fades and the moment passes.
