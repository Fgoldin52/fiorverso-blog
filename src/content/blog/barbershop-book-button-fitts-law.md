A guy is standing outside on a Friday afternoon, phone in one hand, coffee in the other. He found your barbershop, likes the look of it, and wants to grab a chair for tomorrow. He taps his thumb toward the "Book Now" button up in the corner — and just misses it. Taps again. Hits the menu instead. Sighs, locks his phone, and figures he'll do it later.

He won't do it later.

That booking didn't fall through because of your prices, your cuts, or your reviews. It fell through because of where a button was sitting on a screen — and there's a specific, well-documented reason it happened.

## The law hiding in your customer's thumb

Back in the 1950s, a psychologist named Paul Fitts measured how long it takes people to move to and hit a target. He found something so consistent it became a law: the time it takes to reach a target depends on how big it is and how far away it is. Bigger and closer is fast. Smaller and farther is slow — and frustrating.

That sounds obvious until you realize almost every barbershop website is built as if it isn't true.

Here's the part that matters for you. Nearly everyone visiting your site is on a phone, holding it in one hand, tapping with one thumb. And a thumb has a range. Hold your own phone right now and you'll feel it — the bottom and middle of the screen are easy, effortless. The top corners? You either shimmy the phone down your hand or reach across with your other hand. Most people won't bother. They just don't tap.

So when your most important button — the one that turns a curious visitor into a Saturday appointment — is small and sitting up in the top-right corner, you've placed it in the exact spot a one-handed thumb can't comfortably reach.

## What that placement is quietly costing you

The cost here is sneaky because nothing looks broken. The button is *there*. It works. If you test it on your laptop with a mouse, it's fine — a mouse can hit anything instantly, which is exactly why so many sites get designed this way and nobody notices the problem.

But your customers aren't on a laptop with a mouse. They're one-handed on a phone, half-distracted, deciding in a few seconds whether booking is easy enough to bother with. Every extra bit of effort — a reach, a miss, a second attempt — is a moment where they can give up. And on mobile, giving up is just locking the phone. There's no friction to quitting.

You never see these losses. There's no "almost booked" report. The guy outside with the coffee simply isn't in your chair tomorrow, and you have no idea he was ever close.

## The fix takes about thirty seconds to understand

Make the button big, and put it where the thumb already lives.

That's genuinely the whole thing. Your single most valuable action — booking — should be a large, obvious button sitting in the bottom portion of the screen, right in the natural sweep of someone's thumb. Not tucked in a corner. Not shrunk to fit a tidy header. Big, and low, and impossible to miss or fumble.

A good pattern is a booking button that stays "stuck" to the bottom of the screen as people scroll, always within reach no matter where they are on the page. Wherever they decide they're ready, the button is right there under their thumb. No stretching, no aiming, no second attempt.

Same site. Same visitors. Same prices. The only thing that changed is that booking got physically easier — and easier is what gets more people to actually do it.

## Where this leaves you

Most local business sites lose customers not at the big, obvious stuff but at these tiny points of friction — a button half a centimeter out of reach, a tap that takes two tries. Each one feels trivial. Added up across every phone that visits your site, they're a real number of appointments you never booked.

The barbershops that win online aren't doing anything flashy. They just made the next step effortless: one big button, right where the thumb already is, so the guy outside with the coffee books the chair before he ever has a reason to put the phone away.
